# **Research Report: Empirical Markers of AI-Generated Business Prose and Efficacy of Editorial Interventions**

## **Executive Summary**

* **(High Confidence)** Instruction-tuned Large Language Models (LLMs) operate with a structural bias toward "informationally dense, noun-heavy" registers, resulting in the empirically measured overuse of nominalisations (1.5 to 2 times the human baseline) and present participial clauses (2 to 5 times the human baseline)1 .  
* **(High Confidence)** Professional readers and frequent LLM users detect AI-generated text with near-perfect accuracy (e.g., 99.6% accuracy in controlled trials) not merely through vocabulary, but by recognising formulaic discourse structures, lack of syntactic variance, and the absence of organic textual "burstiness"2 .  
* **(High Confidence)** B2B marketing audiences exhibit an "AI Penalty" and a "Disclosure Paradox"; while audiences ethically demand transparency regarding AI authorship, explicit disclosure empirically erodes perceptions of author trustworthiness, competence, caring, and authenticity4 .  
* **(Medium Confidence)** The most pervasive discourse-level marker of AI generation is "templatedness." In empathetic and persuasive contexts, LLMs default to rigid sequence templates (e.g., validation followed by structured advice) that account for 83% to 90% of generated outputs, a homogeneity rarely found in human drafting6 .  
* **(Medium Confidence)** Conventional prompt instructions to "write with enhanced style" reliably trigger a regression to the model's default "AI register." Successful mitigation requires breaking the generation process into "flattening" (neutralisation) followed by constrained, few-shot stylistic reconstruction using negative constraints and strict deterministic parsers8 .  
* **(Low Confidence)** While AI vocabulary and syntax are homogenising scientific and business discourse globally, the ultimate impact on long-term B2B conversion metrics remains unquantified at scale, primarily due to the proprietary nature of corporate pipeline data and the confounding variable of human-in-the-loop editing practices10 .

## **Detailed Findings**

### **Which measurable features of prose most strongly drive professional readers to judge business writing as AI-generated versus human-written, and which interventions demonstrably remove them?**

The detection of AI-generated business prose by professional readers is driven by a confluence of probabilistic linguistic choices that deviate from natural human variance. These features operate across three distinct layers: surface tells (vocabulary), structural tells (syntax and rhythm), and content hollowness (semantic density).  
At the surface layer, professional readers are highly sensitive to the sudden intrusion of "AI vocabulary." This vocabulary is characterised by a shift toward Romance-origin terminology and highly formal, abstract adjectives12 . Words such as "meticulous," "intricate," and "commendable" have experienced frequency spikes of 9.8 to 34.7 times their pre-2023 baselines in academic and professional corpora14 . Expert annotators consistently cite these lexical anomalies—including "delve," "tapestry," and "robust"—as the primary heuristics for immediate suspicion2 .  
However, surface-level vocabulary is merely a symptom of a deeper structural divergence. At the structural layer, AI-generated text is flagged by readers due to its absence of *burstiness*—the natural human tendency to alternate between short, fragmented sentences and long, complex ones15 . Professional readers report that AI prose possesses an unnaturally "pristine" quality that lacks the hesitation, self-correction, or organic tangents characteristic of human thought processes3 . This uniformity is mathematically observable; LLMs rely heavily on rigid syntactic templates, specifically Part-of-Speech (PoS) sequences. Research demonstrates that 76% of syntactic templates in model-generated text mirror the most frequent pre-training data distributions, compared to only 35% in human-authored text18 .  
At the content layer, readers detect AI via the presence of "slop"—a taxonomy of text characterised by high verbosity but low propositional idea density (information-theoretic token entropy)20 . This hollowness manifests when an LLM structurally simulates an argument but fails to populate it with context-specific semantic weight, resulting in generic profundities that could apply to any industry.  
Interventions that successfully remove these markers must move beyond simple stylistic prompting. Attempts to mitigate structural uniformity using zero-shot prompts (e.g., "vary sentence length") routinely fail. Because LLMs are predictive, attempting to force variance via simple text instruction results in overcorrection, creating a detectable "metronome" effect where the AI mechanically alternates between exactly one short fragment and one long sentence. Consequently, the most demonstrably effective interventions utilise deterministic encodability. This involves employing frameworks like Pydantic and Jinja templating to enforce absolute negative constraints (e.g., hard-blocking specific words and syntactic templates) and validating the output prior to final generation8 . Furthermore, the "Agent-Statistics" method—where human notes are first processed into a "flattened," style-less semantic draft before being reconstructed using few-shot, highly constrained brand-voice examples—has been shown to effectively bypass the LLM's default stylistic regression8 .

### **Which linguistic features are overrepresented in LLM-generated prose relative to matched human corpora in published corpus studies (2023 to present): vocabulary, sentence-length distribution, nominalisation, copula avoidance, participial clauses, punctuation habits?**

Corpus linguistics studies from 2023 to the present provide definitive empirical boundaries on the grammatical divergence of LLM text from human corpora. These overrepresented features are heavily influenced by the Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and instruction-tuning phases of model development. Instruction tuning inadvertently penalises stylistic variance and epistemic uncertainty in favour of safe, polished, and informationally dense neutrality1 .  
**Nominalisation and Participial Clauses:** Utilising Douglas Biber's multidimensional analysis framework, Reinhart et al. (2025) demonstrate that instruction-tuned models (such as GPT-4o and Llama 3 Instruct) write in a highly "informationally dense" register that deviates significantly from natural human phrasing1 . LLMs systematically overuse nominalisations—the conversion of verbs or adjectives into noun phrases, such as writing "the implementation of a solution" rather than "implementing a solution"—at 1.5 to 2 times the rate of matched human text. Furthermore, LLMs utilise present participial clauses (e.g., "Leaning on his agility, he evaded the blow") at 2 to 5 times the rate of human authors1 . This produces an artificially dense, noun-heavy syntax that feels structurally correct but stylistically exhausting to professional readers.  
**Copula Avoidance and Lexical Bundles:** Practitioner stylistic analyses observe a distinct "copula avoidance" in generative AI, where models consistently avoid the basic "to be" verbs (is, are, was, were) in favour of inflated, multi-word variants like "serves as," "stands as," or "represents a"23 . Academic corpus analyses corroborate this phenomenon at the structural level. Jiang & Hyland (2024, 2025\) found that in argumentative essays, ChatGPT exhibits a lower overall frequency of 3-word lexical bundles compared to humans, but the bundles it does use possess a higher type/token ratio, rendering its transitional phrases highly rigid and formulaic24 . AI text over-relies on noun- and preposition-based bundles for abstract descriptions and transitional cues. Conversely, human writers naturally employ clause-related bundles that reflect epistemic stance and authorial presence (e.g., "I believe that," "some argue that")24 . LLM prose fundamentally lacks this interactional metadiscourse, resulting in a cold, expository tone even when prompted to be persuasive.  
**Sentence-Length Distribution and Syntactic Complexity:** AI text is statistically smoother and relies on differing methods of clause construction compared to human writers. Studies comparing student essays to AI outputs reveal that while human writers utilise longer Mean Lengths of T-Units (MLT) at 23.61, AI texts average an MLT of 15.9126 . However, AI compensates for shorter sentence units by stacking dependent clauses. ChatGPT demonstrates a dependent clause to T-Unit ratio (DC/T) of 0.75, compared to 0.57 for humans, leading to syntactically complicated sentences that require greater cognitive effort from the reader26 . Furthermore, stylistic variance exists even among leading models. Readability analyses indicate that ChatGPT averages a highly complex Average Sentence Length (ASL) of 37.22 words, skewing toward adverbial modifiers and direct objects, whereas DeepSeek averages an ASL of 26.06, skewing toward nouns and nominal subjects27 . In all cases, LLM sentence length distributions cluster tightly around their respective means with minimal standard deviation, yielding the low burstiness that professional readers subconsciously detect15 .  
\<CONFLICTING\_EVIDENCE\>Regarding the absolute measure of lexical diversity in LLM prose, empirical studies present conflicting conclusions. Gryka et al. (2024) and Jiang & Hyland (2025) report that ChatGPT exhibits *higher* lexical diversity (Type-Token Ratio, MTLD) than human student cohorts15 . Conversely, Miletic & Falk (2026) found that LLM-modified texts in scientific NLP corpora exhibit a *lower* lexical diversity compared to the original human authors29 . This conflict implies that LLM lexical diversity is highly contingent on the baseline domain: LLMs possess broader, more diverse vocabularies than novice students, but narrower, more repetitive vocabularies than expert domain authors.\</CONFLICTING\_EVIDENCE\>

### **Beyond the sentence level, which discourse- and document-level patterns mark AI long-form writing: uniform section templates, density of aphorisms and "quotable" closers, symmetrical contrast constructions ("not X, but Y"), rule-of-three lists, uniform paragraph and entry lengths?**

Beyond the sentence boundary, professional audiences detect AI through predictable macro-structures. LLMs are engineered via reward modeling to fulfil user instructions comprehensively, securely, and neatly. This architectural goal consistently results in exhaustive, symmetrical, and tightly bounded discourse patterns that humans rarely naturally produce.  
**Templatedness and The Rule of Three:** LLMs heavily favour symmetrical tri-part structures at both the sentence and document level. Whether outlining benefits, summarizing key takeaways, or generating headers, the models frequently deploy the "Rule of Three" (e.g., "Speed, efficiency, and innovation") even when a binary comparison or singular focus would be rhetorically superior23 . At the document level, this templating becomes mathematically overwhelming. Gueorguieva et al. (2026) analysed 3,265 AI-generated empathetic responses and discovered that 83% to 90% of them followed an identical, structured sequence of psychological tactics (e.g., validation → self-disclosure → structured advice → supportive closing)6 . In contrast, human responses to the exact same prompts displayed vast structural diversity, indicating that LLMs rely on a singular "modal template" for long-form discourse generation32 .  
**Symmetrical Contrast Constructions and Aphorisms:** Generative AI heavily utilises negative parallelism to feign analytical depth. Structural formats such as "Not just X, but also Y," "It's not X; it's Y," "While X is important, Y is crucial," and "Less X, more Y" are pervasive throughout LLM business prose23 . In B2B marketing, this manifests structurally as forced aphorisms or "quotable closers" at the end of paragraphs, neatly resolving complex, multifaceted topics with a generic profundity (e.g., "Ultimately, X is a testament to the dynamic realm of Y"). Furthermore, AI writers tend to utilise "hook transitions" (e.g., "But here's the kicker:", "That's only half the story.") to bridge paragraphs, rather than organic conceptual flow16 .  
**Homogenisation of Discourse:** At a macro-corpus level, the reliance on these section templates and contrast constructions causes severe "outcome homogenisation." In a 2025 study of scientific peer reviews, Lin & Zhu demonstrated that widespread LLM intervention essentially flattens cultural and stylistic differences10 . Because LLMs favour high-probability continuations based on their training data, they enforce a monocultural cognitive and linguistic template that strips away idiosyncratic human perspectives, leading to uniform paragraph lengths and predictable document flow across entirely distinct authors and industries11 .

### **What do human-perception experiments show about who detects AI text, at what accuracy, and which cues readers explicitly report using, especially expert or heavy-LLM-user readers?**

Empirical studies on human detection of AI text reveal a stark competence gap based on the reader's daily familiarity with LLMs. This data decisively invalidates the common industry premise that "if the copy reads fluently, human readers cannot tell it is AI."  
**Expert vs. Novice Detection Accuracy:** Russell et al. (2025) conducted a rigorous controlled trial where human annotators were tasked with identifying AI-generated articles amidst active evasion tactics (such as paraphrasing and LLM "humanisation" prompts)2 . Annotators who rarely used LLMs performed exceptionally poorly and consistently overestimated their detection capabilities. Conversely, a subset of "expert" annotators—defined as individuals who frequently use LLMs for writing tasks in their daily workflows—achieved near-perfect detection accuracy. The majority vote of five such expert annotators misclassified only 1 out of 300 articles (99.6% accuracy)2 . Remarkably, this human expert consensus significantly outperformed automated open-source AI detectors (e.g., Binoculars, Fast-DetectGPT), highlighting that heavy LLM users develop highly sensitive, intuitive pattern recognition that surpasses current probabilistic algorithms34 .  
**Reported Cues:** When qualitative feedback was gathered, expert readers reported a hierarchy of cues. While they easily identified specific lexical tells—categorised as "AI vocabulary" (e.g., *crucial*, *vibrant*, *significantly*, *underscore*)—their primary and most reliable detection mechanisms were structural and tonal2 . Experts noted formulaic document structures, such as "optimistically vague introductions and conclusions," and an over-polished cadence3 . Furthermore, readers in separate disclosure studies reported detecting a tangible "loss of human touch and sincerity," highlighting the AI's tendency to display diminished authorial effort, stylistic awkwardness, and an inability to correctly contextualise emotional weight5 .

### **What evidence exists on the commercial effects of perceived AI authorship in B2B marketing: trust, credibility, engagement, conversion, including AI-disclosure studies?**

In the B2B marketing and fintech sectors, credibility, authoritative domain expertise, and relational trust are the primary currencies of conversion. The detection—or explicit disclosure—of AI authorship triggers severe, empirically measured commercial and social penalties.  
**The AI Penalty and Disclosure Paradox:** Sahebi, Formosa, and Bankins (2026) conducted a pre-registered experimental survey (N=547) evaluating recipient responses to business and interpersonal communications authored by humans versus AI4 . They identified a profound "AI Penalty" wherein text attributed to AI is perceived as less trustworthy, less authentic, and crucially, less useful for "knowledge uptake" (the willingness of the business reader to rely on and act upon the information). Furthermore, they identified a concerning "Disclosure Paradox": audiences state ethically that it is highly important for organisations to disclose AI use, yet when organisations *do* transparently disclose it, the audience heavily penalises the communication. This paradox creates perverse commercial incentives for B2B marketers to actively hide AI usage to maintain conversion metrics37 .  
**Erosion of Competence and Caring:** Corroborating the AI Penalty, Nakano et al. (2025) studied 261 participants across 990 evaluations of varied communicative acts. The disclosure of AI authorship caused precipitous declines in perceived author trustworthiness, competence, caring, and likability5 . Thematic feedback indicated this penalty stems from algorithmic aversion rooted in expectations of human effort; readers equate communicative value with the effort expended by the author36 . When an algorithm generates the text, the perceived relational intent is nullified. Similarly, Lehrer et al. (2024) demonstrated via crowdsourced experiments that labelling social media content as AI-generated significantly reduced both affective and behavioural engagement, particularly for emotionally resonant or value-driven messaging39 .  
**Reputation over Messaging in B2B Frameworks:** From a broader marketing strategy perspective, modern B2B purchasing agents increasingly route trust through verifiable human reputation rather than corporate messaging. As AI generation reduces the cost of content production to zero, thought leadership requires observable, accountable human judgement41 . ### **Which editing, prompting or constraint techniques are demonstrated to reduce AI markers, and which merely mask them or overcorrect into new detectable patterns (for example metronomic short/long sentence alternation)?**

To safely leverage LLMs for B2B ghostwriting without triggering the AI Penalty among expert readers, prompt engineering must evolve from simple tonal requests into strict, programmatic architectural constraints.  
**Ineffective Techniques and Overcorrection Risks:** Simple, qualitative zero-shot prompts—such as "Write more like a human," "Be persuasive," or "Vary your sentence length"—fail reliably. Because LLMs operate via statistical prediction, attempting to force stylistic variance through vague text instruction results in overcorrection. For example, commanding an LLM to vary its rhythm frequently induces a "metronome rhythm"—a highly detectable, alternating pattern of one short, punchy fragment followed immediately by one excessively long, complex sentence, repeated ad infinitum23 . Furthermore, research on LLM evaluation behavior reveals that instructing LLMs to rigorously double-check logic or apply complex analytical constraints can lead to severe overcorrection and high false-negative rates. The model begins hallucinating flaws in perfectly sound structures simply to satisfy the prompt's implied demand for critique43 .  
**Effective Editing and Prompting Interventions:**

1. **The Flatten-and-Reconstruct Pipeline:** The most effective structural intervention avoids using LLMs to draft directly from zero-shot prompts. Instead, workflows should use LLMs to extract "flattened" semantic data from human notes, intentionally stripping away the model's default stylistic embellishments. Subsequently, a highly constrained, few-shot prompting phase is used to reconstruct the text based purely on strict brand-voice templates and validated examples8 .  
2. **Deterministic Encodability (Pydantic and Jinja):** Style rules must be enforced programmatically rather than qualitatively. By utilising schema validation frameworks like Pydantic paired with Jinja templates, parameters (e.g., maximum sentence length, exclusion of target vocabulary, forced sentence case) can be defined as code contracts. The output is validated at runtime, forcing the LLM to rewrite if it fails the structural lint check before any tokens reach the end-user8 .  
3. **Negative Constraints:** Defining exactly what the brand voice *is not* is highly effective for bounding LLM outputs. Supplying a specific "anti-ai-writing-style" markdown file within the context window that contains explicitly banned words (e.g., "delve," "leverage," "tapestry") and banned syntactic patterns (e.g., "Not X, but Y") successfully limits both surface and structural tells23 .

### **What do professional editors and style authorities at named business publications describe as the craft of rhythm, emphasis, specificity and restraint in expert-audience prose?**

Expert editorial standards from legacy business publishers serve as the ultimate antidote to LLM "slop." Translating these standards into LLM constraints requires understanding the human mechanics of rhetorical restraint, specificity, and data density.  
**The Economist:** The *Economist Style Guide* champions George Orwell's rules for plain English, demanding the ruthless excision of jargon, pre-packaged metaphors, and unnecessary words. Crucially, it dictates absolute rhetorical restraint: "Do not be hectoring or arrogant... Nobody needs to be described as silly: let your analysis show that he is." It specifically bans "chatty" transitions (e.g., "Surprise, surprise," "Wait for it"), which LLMs often hallucinate when vaguely prompted to be "engaging" or "conversational"44 . The emphasis is on letting the facts persuade rather than inflating the prose.  
**McKinsey & Company:** The standard McKinsey communication style relies on radical specificity and strict structural formatting. Sentences must be simple and entirely jargon-free. Every bullet point must begin with an active, past-tense verb and contain a rigorously quantified metric. Fluff, vague duties, and corporate "puffery" (e.g., "responsible for managing a team") are entirely forbidden, demanding a remarkably high data-to-ink ratio46 . This contrasts sharply with LLMs, which default to abstract nouns and unquantified ranges.  
**Boston Consulting Group (BCG):** BCG champions a philosophy called "Smart Simplicity." In presentations and reports, text is aggressively minimised to support visual data. Action titles are restricted to absolute claims under 15-20 words, leading with a number where relevant, and never relying on vague observational statements47 .  
To effectively mimic expert human prose, LLMs must be strictly constrained to present raw, specific data (McKinsey), avoid rhetorical inflation and subjective judgements (The Economist), and allow the reader to deduce the magnitude of the insight without explicitly telling them it is a "seismic shift" or a "pivotal moment" (BCG).

## **Recommended Next Steps**

1. **Implement an Automated "AI-Slop" Linter:** Integrate a programmatic linting tool into the marketing team's CMS architecture. This tool should use regex and PoS tagging to flag the known symmetrical contrast patterns, alert on high densities of present participial clauses (\>15%), and hard-block identified "AI vocabulary."  
   * *Rationale:* Surface and structural tells can be caught deterministically before human editorial review, saving resources and standardising output.  
2. **Develop an "Agent-Statistics" Flattening Pipeline:** Update the LLM ghostwriting SOP to prevent direct zero-shot drafting. Require marketers to pass source material through a "flattening" prompt to strip AI stylistic defaults, followed by a Pydantic-constrained reconstruction prompt injected with approved brand voice examples aligned with McKinsey/Economist restraint parameters.  
   * *Rationale:* Zero-shot drafting is the root cause of the "AI Penalty"; constrained reconstruction forces the model to mimic specified structural variance.  
3. **Audit Existing Content for the "Disclosure Paradox" Risk:** Review current B2B marketing assets (investor whitepapers, case studies) for obvious AI generation markers. Due to the "Disclosure Paradox," if content *feels* AI-generated but is not disclosed, or *is* disclosed, trust plummets.  
   * *Rationale:* High-stakes investor relations content must either be verified human-authored (and marked as such to leverage reputation) or heavily edited to completely remove detectable AI structural tells to avoid the AI Penalty.
