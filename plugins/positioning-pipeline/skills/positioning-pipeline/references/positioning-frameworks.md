# Positioning frameworks — the four books, distilled

Load this before authoring research prompts or synthesising territories. Each book gives you specific *moves* and *failure modes*. Use all four; they constrain each other.

---

## 1 · Ries & Trout — *Positioning: The Battle for Your Mind*

**Core idea.** Positioning is not what you do to the product; it's what you do to the *mind of the prospect*. The mind is small, hates complexity, and holds roughly one idea per brand. You win by occupying an *open slot* in the mind and owning it.

**The moves:**
- **Own one word.** A brand that means many things means nothing. Find the single word/idea you can own.
- **Be first, or create a category you can be first in.** If you can't lead an existing category, reframe to one you can lead.
- **Position *against* the leader.** Name an enemy. "The X for people who hate Y." An enemy gives the prospect an instant mental slot for you.
- **Beware the line-extension trap.** Every additional thing you ask the brand to stand for *subtracts* from the original idea's strength. Breadth, *led with*, destroys the slot.

**Failure mode to avoid:** the "everything" pitch. "Everything-app / all-in-one" occupies no slot and reads as bloatware. Keep the ambition internal; lead with one word.

**In the synthesis:** give each territory exactly **one word to own** and **one named enemy**.

---

## 2 · April Dunford — *Obviously Awesome*

**Core idea.** Customers understand a *new* thing only through the *context* (market category) you give them. The category sets expectations about competitors, price, and which features matter. If you don't choose the frame, the prospect picks one — badly — and your best features go invisible.

**The five (+1) components — assemble these for every position:**
1. **Competitive alternatives** — what would the customer do if you didn't exist? (Rarely "nothing.")
2. **Unique attributes** — what you have that the alternatives don't (features/capabilities).
3. **Value (+ proof)** — the benefit those attributes enable that customers care about.
4. **Target-market characteristics** — who cares *a lot* and buys quickly.
5. **Market category** — the frame that makes your value obvious.
6. **(+) Relevant trend** — ride a wave, but only one that reinforces a durable attribute.

**The category choice (decisive):** (a) win the head of an existing category, (b) **win a subsegment** of one, or (c) create a new category. Category creation is expensive — usually reserve it. The pragmatic play is **(b): anchor in an understood category, then re-segment** so your strengths become obvious.

**Test:** a cold prospect should *get it in ~10 seconds*. If the category label needs explaining, it's wrong.

**In the synthesis:** specify all six components for each territory; pick the category frame deliberately.

## 3 · Kim & Mauborgne — *Blue Ocean Strategy*

**Core idea.** Stop competing in crowded "red oceans" on the same factors. Create uncontested space via **value innovation** (differentiate *and* lower cost) by changing *which factors* you compete on.

**The Four Actions (ERRC) — build one per territory:**
- **Eliminate** — which factors the industry takes for granted should be removed?
- **Reduce** — which should be cut well below the standard?
- **Raise** — which should be raised well above the standard?
- **Create** — which factors the industry never offered should be created?

**Strategy canvas:** a winning value curve has **Focus** (you go all-in on a few factors), **Divergence** (it looks unlike the field), and a **compelling tagline**. A curve that's "present on everything" has none of these — it reads as undifferentiated breadth.

**In the synthesis:** give each territory an ERRC, name its empty "square," and a one-line tagline.

---

## 4 · David C. Baker — *The Business of Expertise*

**Core idea.** Authority, pricing power, and inbound demand flow to the **specialist**, not the generalist. Expertise is pattern-matching inside a *narrow* domain. "Everything for everyone" forfeits the premium.

**The moves:**
- **Specialise — horizontal (a discipline) or vertical (an audience)** — until it's almost uncomfortable. The narrower the focus, the more authority the market hands you.
- A broad *product* can still have a narrow *positioning*: specialise on the **who/job**, even if the product does many things.
- Narrowing is not giving up the bigger market — it's the credible *path* to it (land, then expand).

**In the synthesis:** each territory names a sharp **beachhead** (the who that feels the pain most acutely and buys fastest), and you say who you're *not* for.

## How the four resolve together (the standard synthesis)

The books pull in two directions — Blue Ocean rewards bold breadth; Ries/Trout and Baker demand narrow focus. **Resolve it the same way every time: position narrow, deliver broad.** Lead with one wedge (one word, one enemy, one beachhead, one category) that gets you into the mind and the door; let the breadth be the *expansion/moat* story revealed *after* adoption, never the opening line. Every successful platform did this (Amazon=books, Notion=docs, Linear=issue tracking, Superhuman=fastest email, Figma=design-in-the-browser).

**The repeated failure mode across all four:** leading with the feature list / "all-in-one." It destroys the mind-slot (Ries/Trout), invites a red-ocean frame (Dunford), produces a no-focus value curve (Blue Ocean), and forfeits the specialist premium (Baker). Catch it every time.
