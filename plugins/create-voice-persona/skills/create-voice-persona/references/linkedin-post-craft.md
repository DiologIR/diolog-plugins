# LinkedIn Post Craft — Research Base

Synthesised from LinkedIn engagement deep research (2024–2026: algorithm behaviour, format performance, readability practice). This is the **default research layer** for the LinkedIn variant of any generated voice persona. If the user supplies their own deep-research document, read it alongside this file: their research **adds to and, where it conflicts, overrides** this one (it is fresher and often audience-specific). Fold the merged findings into the generated `linkedin-post.md` variant so the persona package stands alone.

Everything here is craft, not voice. The person's voice always wins over a tactic: a post that sounds exactly like them and lands quietly beats a slick post that sounds like anyone. That trade-off is not hypothetical — the algorithm itself now down-ranks generic AI-flavoured content, so authenticity is the engagement strategy.

## What the algorithm rewards

- **Relevance + expertise + meaningful engagement, not virality.** LinkedIn surfaces knowledge-rich content from credible people to a relevant audience; generic viral bait is suppressed. Value to a specific audience is the first filter: decide what knowledge, insight, or usable takeaway the post gives before writing a word.
- **Comments over likes.** Substantive comments (15+ words) carry roughly 2× the reach weight of short ones. The closing question exists to earn real comments, not "Great post 👏".
- **Dwell time.** Content that holds attention (a strong hook, a story, a carousel, a genuinely useful long read) gets distributed wider.
- **The golden hour.** Engagement in the first 60–120 minutes decides distribution; posts inviting an immediate, specific reaction do better.
- **Native content.** Formats that keep users on-platform win. External links go in the **first comment**, never the post body; say "link in the first comment" in the post.
- **Authenticity.** Un-personalised AI-generated content gets reduced reach. A real voice with lived experience is the moat — which is the entire reason a voice persona is worth building.

## The hook (first 1–3 lines)

Only ~140–200 characters show before "…see more", and ~82% of readers decide there whether to expand. The hook must earn the click alone. Shapes that work:

- A bold or slightly contrarian statement from experience
- A sharp, specific observation
- A concrete number or result that raises an eyebrow
- A short personal admission or the opening beat of a story
- A direct question naming a real tension the audience feels
- A direct address of a known pain point

Avoid: throat-clearing ("I've been thinking a lot about…"), definitions, clickbait, and hooks that promise more than the body delivers. The hook is written in the person's voice like everything else — a manufactured "hooky" register is its own AI tell.

## Structure and readability

- Hook (1–3 lines) → body (the insight, story, or argument) → **one** genuine closing question.
- Short paragraphs, 1–3 sentences, line breaks for white space; most readers are on mobile.
- Bullets or a short numbered list only when genuinely breaking down steps or options — not as the default shape (see `ai-writing-signs.md` on canned list formats).
- Storytelling arc (hook → journey/challenge → lesson) makes content relatable and memorable when the person has a real story; never fabricate one.
- **Length:** ~150–400 words tends to over-perform for feed engagement (under ~300 often best); up to 3,000 characters is allowed and longer is fine only when packed with value and well formatted. Communicate the value in as few words as it takes.
- LinkedIn renders **no markdown**: no `**bold**`, no `##` headings. Emphasis comes from structure and word choice.

## Features

- **Hashtags:** 3–5 relevant, at the end (or woven naturally), PascalCase (#InvestorRelations #Fintech), mixing one or two broad with niche. More than 5 reads as spam.
- **@ Mentions:** only directly relevant people/companies, ideally under 5, and only where the tag adds context (credit, genuine invitation into the discussion).
- **CTA:** exactly one, and it is a focused open question inviting a specific experience or perspective ("What's your biggest challenge with X?"). Action verbs are fine; engagement bait is not ("Like if you agree", "Repost this") — the algorithm penalises it and it sounds like no actual person.
- **Polls:** direct question, clear options, an "Other — see comments" option to spur discussion.

## Format ranking (for the accompanying visual)

By average engagement: multi-image / PDF carousel (highest, strong dwell time; design each slide to stand alone) > native video (square/vertical for mobile, subtitles always, hook in the first seconds, usually under 90s) > single high-quality non-generic image (~2× the comment rate of text-only) > poll (highest impressions) > text-only (lowest reach, but excellent when the writing carries a strong opinion or story).

## Long-form articles

Built for depth and authority rather than feed engagement. Sweet spot ~1,500–2,200 words. A title that sets a specific expectation; the same hook discipline in the first paragraph; clear sentence-case headings; one idea per section; a pulled-out stat or worked example as a visual anchor; a conclusion that lands the takeaway and ends on a question or a plain next thought — never an "In conclusion" restatement.

## Timing and cadence (context only)

Weekday mid-morning to mid-afternoon, Tue/Wed/Thu strongest; weekends weakest; sustainable cadence 2–5×/week. Mention only if the user asks about when to post — the persona's job is the writing.

## Anti-patterns (these get encoded in every generated LinkedIn variant)

- Engagement bait, keyword stuffing, >5 hashtags, irrelevant tagging.
- External link in the post body.
- Salesy CTA or feature pitch where a question should be.
- "Broetry" (one line per sentence for its own sake), manufactured vulnerability, humblebrag framing.
- Every AI tell in `ai-writing-signs.md` — the algorithm and the audience both punish them.
