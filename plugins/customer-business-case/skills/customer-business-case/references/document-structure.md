# Document structure (content-neutral)

This is the **argument shape and messaging discipline** for a Diolog customer
proposal, distilled from the standard Diolog deck-build brief and de-identified.
It is a **STRUCTURE reference, not a content source.** Use it for *what kind of
point goes where* and *what to emphasise vs soften*. Do NOT copy any wording,
figures, or facts from here or from any example into a client's document. Every
concrete claim, number, price and name on the page comes from **that client's
customer deck / proposal** (the runtime content source), written in the Diolog
brand voice.

Diolog is the fixed vendor. The reader is a **CFO / board / CEO** at a lean
listed (or pre-listing) company that does not run a large in-house IR, design or
web team. The document must make a commercial argument and fit on one A4 page.

## The four outcomes every point ladders back to

Whatever the client, each section should serve one of these. If a point serves
none of them, cut it.

1. A more professional, credible market-facing presence.
2. Less senior time spent on low-value formatting and production.
3. A better investor portal / website experience, without a full rebuild.
4. Earlier compliance and disclosure-issue detection before content goes out.

## The argument arc (maps onto the 8 document sections)

The one-pager compresses a full proposal into this arc. The mapping to the A4
template's numbered sections is shown in brackets.

1. **Positioning / the promise** — one line: Diolog lifts the professionalism of
   investor-facing materials, cuts production time, modernises the investor web
   presence, and adds a disclosure-aware review layer, without a big internal
   team. *(→ Header + 01 The case)*
2. **The problem** — the client's constraints, framed without implying they are
   behind. Usually a lean team carrying production + disclosure load, and being
   presented to the market below their true quality. *(→ 02 The problem, exactly
   three constraints)*
3. **The solution** — introduce Diolog: a governed workspace holding the
   company's history, brand and disclosures in one context. Typically three
   capabilities: **Presentation Studio** (decks, results, FAQs, talking points
   from the company's own materials), **Investor Portal** (a professional
   investor centre without a website rebuild), **Compliance Guardian** (pre-
   publication disclosure/consistency checks). *(→ 03 The solution, the one navy
   card)*
4. **The value** — the shifts in how the company shows up: lower disclosure risk,
   consistent voice, senior hours back to strategy, credible investor presence.
   *(→ 04 The value, four columns)*
5. **The quantified outcome** — quantify twice: time saved per cycle (before /
   with Diolog / saved) and money saved (what Diolog replaces). *(→ 05 The
   outcome table + 06 What it replaces)*
6. **The investment** — the recommended plan, price and cadence, with a short
   feature checklist. *(→ 07 Investment, wash card)*
7. **The next step** — one specific, easy-to-approve ask (a booking / pilot), one
   CTA. Not a menu. *(→ 08 Next steps, wash card + CTA)*

## Messaging guardrails (generic Diolog positioning)

**Emphasise:** professionalisation and polish · reduced design/document
production time · CFO confidence and external perception · website / investor
portal uplift · disclosure-aware review before content goes out · a lean listed-
company operating model · the adviser moving from *doing* to strategic advisory ·
institutional credibility and register diversification.

**Avoid or soften:** high-volume investor-inbox language · retail-investor surge
language · "always-on engagement" as the main ROI · any claim that Diolog
replaces financial modelling · overpromising cold investor targeting · implying
the client has a large IR team · making the client's current website sound
terrible · generic AI-productivity messaging.

## Compression rules

- **Rule of three / four:** three problems, four value shifts, four-ish outcome
  rows. Tight, parallel, scannable.
- **Quantify twice** (time and money) and let the two totals be the payoff the
  eye lands on.
- **Less is more.** One page holds the argument, not the appendix. No filler
  sections, no data slop, nothing that does not change the reader's decision.
- **Lead with the CFO pain**, then Diolog as the practical answer: create the
  materials, publish them into a stronger portal, check them before they go out.
