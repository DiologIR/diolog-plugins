# LinkedIn & Long-Form Engagement Reference

Distilled from an 18-month research synthesis (2024–2025 LinkedIn algorithm + content best practice). This is the layer that makes a well-voiced article actually *land*. Apply the drafting-relevant parts; the writer controls the words, not the posting schedule, so timing/analytics notes are kept brief at the end.

## What the algorithm now rewards (so the writing serves it)

- **Relevance + expertise + meaningful engagement**, not virality. The platform surfaces knowledge-rich content from credible people to a relevant audience. Generic viral bait is suppressed.
- **Comments over likes.** Substantive comments (15+ words) are roughly twice as powerful for reach as short ones. The whole point of the closing question is to earn real comments, not "Great post 👏".
- **Dwell time.** Content that holds attention (a strong hook, a story, a carousel, a genuinely useful long read) gets pushed wider. Hook them, then reward the click.
- **The golden hour.** Early engagement in the first 60–120 minutes decides distribution. Posts that invite an immediate, specific reaction do better.
- **Native + authentic.** Keep people on-platform; put any external link in the **first comment**, not the post body. AI-generated, un-personalised content gets reduced reach — which is exactly why Luke's real voice and lived experience are the moat.

## The hook (first 1–3 lines / ~140–200 characters)

This is the most important sentence you write. ~82% of readers decide whether to expand on the first lines, and only ~140–200 characters show before "…see more". The hook must earn the click on its own.

Strong hook shapes (pick what fits Luke's stance, keep it in his plain voice):
- A bold or slightly contrarian statement from experience ("A year ago I rejected most of what AI coding tools suggested. Now I reject less, but I've gotten pickier about where I let them off the leash.")
- A sharp, specific observation about the topic
- A concrete number or result that raises an eyebrow
- A short personal admission / the start of a story
- A direct question that names a real tension the audience feels

Avoid hooks that are throat-clearing ("I've been thinking a lot about…"), clickbait, or a definition.

## Structure

**LinkedIn feed post:**
- Hook (1–3 lines) → body (the insight/story/argument) → one genuine question to close.
- Short paragraphs, 1–3 sentences, with line breaks for white space (most readers are on mobile).
- Bullets or a short numbered list when breaking down steps or options.
- 3–5 relevant hashtags at the end, PascalCase (e.g. #InvestorRelations #Fintech), mixing one or two broad with niche.
- Length: ~150–400 words (under ~300 words tends to over-perform for feed engagement; up to ~2,000 characters is fine for a thought-leadership point). Communicate the value in as few words as possible.
- One CTA only: a focused, open question inviting a specific experience or perspective. No sell, no "DM me", no feature pitch.

**LinkedIn article / long-form blog:**
- Built for depth and authority, not immediate feed engagement. Sweet spot ~1,500–2,200 words (LinkedIn Articles peak around 1,900–2,200).
- A title that sets a clear, specific expectation (avoid clickbait; a how-to or a pointed claim works).
- A strong opening that frames the problem or the stakes in the first paragraph (same hook discipline — earn the read).
- Clear section headings; one idea per section; short paragraphs and white space still apply even in long form.
- Use lists, a pulled-out stat, or a short example to break up prose and create visual anchors.
- A genuine conclusion that lands the takeaway and ends on a question or a clear, non-salesy next thought.
- If there's a canonical link (the company blog, a source), reference it naturally; on LinkedIn the link goes in the first comment.

## Format ranking (for choosing the accompanying visual)

By average engagement: multi-image / PDF carousel (highest) > native video > single image > poll (highest impressions) > text-only (lowest, but excellent when the writing carries a strong opinion or story). A single, on-brand image lifts comment rate ~2x over text-only. This is why the skill always proposes a graphic concept — see `graphic-concepting.md`.

## Tone (this aligns exactly with Luke's voice)

Authentic, conversational, value-first. Share real experience, lessons, even what went wrong. Help/inform/provoke-thought rather than sell. Shift "here's our product" to "here's what we learned". Over-promotion kills reach and reads as inauthentic — so does AI-flavoured polish.

## Briefly: timing & cadence (context, not the writer's job)

Best windows are weekday mid-morning to mid-afternoon, Tue/Wed/Thu strongest; weekends weakest. Sustainable cadence is 2–5x/week. Mention these only if the user asks about *when* to post — the skill's job is the article itself.
